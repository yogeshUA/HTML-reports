Theory

According to Adkins Reginald[2], the techniques used to deal with conflicts are based on many variables such as our basic underlying temperament, personality, environment, and where we are in our professional career.

Validity and reliability

Cronbach’s coefficient alpha for the conflict management styles questionnaire was found to be highly reliable (α = 0.86), and factor analyses supported the factor structure of the scale.

Meaning of factors

The Conflict Management Styles can be clubbed together into the following five major styles. None of these strategies are superior in and of itself.

Collaborating

Working through differences will lead to creative solutions that will satisfy both parties' concerns.

Avoiding

Avoiding conflict by withdrawing, sidestepping, or postponing.

Compromising

Both ends are placed against the middle in an attempt to serve the "common good," while ensuring each person can maintain something of their original position.

Accommodating

Appease others by downplaying conflict, thus protecting the relationship.

Competing

When goals are extremely important, one must sometimes use power to win.

Compromising

Both ends are placed against the middle in an attempt to serve the "common good," while ensuring each person can maintain something of their original position. They believe that winning something while losing a little is a fair deal. It can be seen as the middle-ground approach.

Compromisers are moderately concerned with both their goals and their relationships with others. They typically seek a compromise; they give up part of their goals and persuade the other person in a conflict to give up part of their goals. They seek a conflict solution in which both sides gain something, the middle ground between two extreme positions. They are willing to sacrifice part of their goals in order to find agreement for the common good.
