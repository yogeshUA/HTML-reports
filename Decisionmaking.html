Decision Making

Theory

According to Y. Leykin, and R. J. DeRubeis, we have an inclination toward specific decision making styles, most natural to us, based on how we think about ourselves and the world. This questionnaire measures nine such decision making styles through 43 items. Each item contains a statement describing the thoughts and behavior while making decisions. Respondents rate how often they indulge in the behavior described in each statement on a five-point scale from Never to Always

Meaning of factor

The Decision Styles Questionnaire was developed using nine decision-making styles to predict how individuals process ideas and thoughts while making decisions.

Anxious

Characterized by high levels of anxiety and fear when making decisions.

Avoidant

Characterized by hesitation, procrastination, and fear of the negative outcome.

Brooding

Characterized by a preoccupation with negative thoughts and feelings when making decisions.

Intuitive

Characterized by reliance on gut feelings, instincts, and emotions when making decisions, rather than on facts and data.

Spontaneous

Characterized by a quick, impulsive, and intuitive decision-making process.

Vigilant

Characterized by a careful and systematic approach to decision-making, a thorough examination of all options, and potential consequences before making a choice.

Respected

Characterized by the perception of trust others have in an individual's ability to make good decisions and handle the consequences that may arise from them.

Confident

Characterized by trust in one's own abilities and judgment when making decisions.

Leykin, Y., & DeRubeis, R. J. (2010). Decision-making styles and depressive symptomatology: Development of the Decision Styles Questionnaire. Judgment and Decision Making, 5(7), 506–515.
